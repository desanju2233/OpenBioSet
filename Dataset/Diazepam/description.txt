Protocol details:

ASSAY TYPE: Competitive ELISA for benzodiazepine-class drugs (including diazepam) in urine or
blood, using a pre-coated antibody microplate. This version of the workflow has been adapted
to run as a sequence of droplet operations on a digital microfluidic biochip (DMFB) instead
of a standard 96-well plate.

REAGENTS USED:
  - Urine sample (diluted specimen)
  - Benzodiazepine-enzyme conjugate (drug labeled with horseradish peroxidase)
  - Distilled water (wash diluent)
  - TMB substrate (3,3',5,5'-tetramethylbenzidine)
  - Stop solution (dilute acid)

STEPS:

1. Combine 10 units of the diluted urine sample with 100 units of the benzodiazepine-enzyme
   conjugate. Mix for 60 seconds.

2. Incubate the combined mixture at 23C (room temperature) for 60 minutes. During this time,
   any free benzodiazepine in the sample competes with the enzyme-labeled conjugate for a
   limited number of antibody binding sites — this competition is the basis of the test.

3. Wash the reaction six times: each wash cycle combines 350 units of distilled water with the
   reaction droplet, mixes for 45 seconds, and removes unbound material before moving to
   detection.

4. Add 100 units of TMB substrate to the washed reaction and mix for 60 seconds. Incubate at
   23C for 30 minutes to allow the color reaction to develop.

5. Add 100 units of stop solution and mix for 60 seconds to halt color development and shift
   the color from blue to yellow.

6. Measure the resulting absorbance signal to obtain the reading. Discard the sample
   afterward.

INTERPRETATION: This is a one-step competitive immunoassay. Free benzodiazepine in the sample
and the enzyme-labeled conjugate compete for a fixed number of antibody binding sites on the
plate. After washing away anything unbound, the remaining bound conjugate is revealed by the
TMB color reaction. Less color development means more drug was present in the sample
(competing more successfully for binding sites); more color development means less or no drug
was present. A result is typically called positive if the sample's absorbance reading is at or
below the reading from a positive reference standard, and negative if it is above.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
A NOTE ON HOW CLOSELY THIS MATCHES THE CITED SOURCE
------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This is an exact numeric match to the cited source across every value: sample volume (10
units), conjugate volume (100 units), first incubation time (60 minutes), wash count and
volume (6 times, 350 units), substrate volume (100 units), second incubation time (30
minutes), and stop solution volume (100 units) all match the source exactly. The only
difference is that the droplet program records the result using the generic "fluorescence"
detect type, since BioScript has no dedicated absorbance-reading instruction, whereas the
real procedure reads absorbance at a dual wavelength (450 nm and 650 nm). This is a known
expressiveness gap in the droplet language, not a deviation from the real procedure.

One caveat on scope: this kit is raised against and validated for benzodiazepines as a drug
class (the antiserum is directed against Oxazepam, with cross-reactivity across the
benzodiazepine group, which includes diazepam), rather than being diazepam-exclusive. This
does not affect the validity of the step-by-step procedure above, which applies identically
regardless of which specific benzodiazepine is being screened for.

An earlier version of this protocol's droplet program included a second, separate
antibody/enzyme incubation-and-wash stage (resembling a two-step "sandwich" ELISA). No real
benzodiazepine or other small-molecule drug immunoassay kit was found using that structure: a
two-step format requires the target molecule to be large enough for two antibodies to bind it
simultaneously, which a small drug molecule (a "hapten") is not large enough to support. Every
real urine drug-screening kit checked (morphine, fentanyl, oxycodone, benzodiazepines) uses
the one-step competitive format reflected in the steps above. The two-step version is treated
as a non-representative artifact of the original test file, not a valid alternative protocol.
